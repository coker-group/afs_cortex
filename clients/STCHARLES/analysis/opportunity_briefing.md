## Executive Summary

St. Charles Health System is a $1.4B (FY2025 operating revenue) community health system in Central Oregon operating hospitals in Bend, Redmond, Madras, and Prineville (the latter two as Critical Access Hospitals). Revenue has grown at a **9.8% CAGR** from $826M (FY2019) to $1.45B (FY2025), driven by patient service volume expansion and premium revenue growth.

However, this topline growth masks a **structurally deteriorating operating margin** — from 4.1% in FY2019 to just **0.30% in FY2025**. The system is running a revenue-expense scissors pattern where expense growth consistently outpaces revenue growth, with labor and supply costs consuming an ever-larger share of revenue. Total bottom-line results remain positive only because of investment returns ($108M in FY2025) and a $24.6M gain on acquisition — neither of which is sustainable or operational.

**This is a system that is growing its way into deeper trouble.** The bigger it gets, the thinner its margins become, and it is increasingly dependent on non-operating income to stay solvent. This creates a compelling multi-workstream consulting opportunity estimated at **$35M–$70M in annual improvement potential**.

## VIII. Diagnostic Summary: Structural Issues vs. Cyclical

### Structural (Multi-Year Trends — Core Consulting Targets)

1. **Revenue-expense scissors** — Operating expenses are growing ~70bps/year faster than revenue. At the current trajectory, the operating margin will turn permanently negative within 2–3 years. This is the #1 strategic risk.

2. **Labor cost overhang** — At 58.3% of revenue, labor costs are 330bps above the A-rated benchmark ceiling. The benefits component ($164M, 11.3% of revenue) is growing especially fast, suggesting healthcare cost inflation for the system's own employees.

3. **Supply cost explosion** — Supplies at 32.8% of revenue is the single largest line item anomaly. Even accounting for definitional differences, the trajectory (28.8% → 32.8% in 6 years) indicates structural procurement and utilization problems.

4. **Thin operating margin dependency on non-operating income** — The system reported $4.4M operating income on $1.45B revenue (FY2025) but $142M excess of revenue over expenses. The $138M gap is investment returns ($108M) and acquisition gain ($25M). Reliance on market returns to fund operations is inherently fragile.

### Cyclical / Now Resolved

5. **COVID-era cash flow disruption** (FY2020–FY2022) — Medicare advance repayments, staffing premiums, and supply shortages created two years of negative operating cash flow. These effects have largely washed through.

6. **Deferred capital investment** (FY2020–FY2023) — Now being addressed with aggressive capex ($173M over FY2024–FY2025). The question is whether this pace is sustainable or another boom-bust cycle.

## IX. Consulting Opportunity Sizing

### Workstream 1: Labor Optimization ($21M–$43M annual)
**Target:** Reduce labor-to-revenue from 58.3% to 55.3–56.3%
- Workforce productivity benchmarking (FTEs per adjusted patient day)
- Travel nurse / premium labor elimination strategy
- Benefits plan design review — $164M benefits on $680M salaries (24% load) is high
- Scope-of-practice optimization for advanced practice providers
- Shared services consolidation across the four hospital campuses

### Workstream 2: Supply Chain & Pharmacy ($15M–$29M annual)
**Target:** Reduce supplies-to-revenue by 100–200bps
- First priority: **Definitional reconciliation** — understand what's included in "Medical supplies, drugs, and other" vs. peers
- GPO contract review and renegotiation (is SCHS in a strong GPO position for its size?)
- Pharmacy formulary management and 340B optimization
- Surgical preference card standardization
- Implant pricing benchmarking

### Workstream 3: Revenue Enhancement ($5M–$10M annual)
**Target:** Sustain top-line momentum while protecting margins
- Payer mix intelligence — Medicare/Medicaid vs. commercial share not available in callouts; recommend supplemental data request
- Premium revenue strategy — the health plan is a differentiator; optimization of risk adjustment and medical loss ratio
- CAH reimbursement optimization for Madras and Prineville facilities
- Capture rate analysis for service lines leaking to Portland/Eugene

### Workstream 4: Capital Planning & Facility Strategy ($3M–$5M cost avoidance)
**Target:** Normalize capital spending to avoid boom-bust cycles
- Multi-year capital plan tied to strategic facility master plan
- Equipment lifecycle management (the FY2025 $118M capex spike suggests compressed replacement)
- Construction cost management for the ongoing Bend/Redmond expansion
- Lease-vs-buy analysis for the expanding ROU portfolio ($43M operating leases)

### Workstream 5: Financial Resilience ($2M–$5M)
**Target:** Reduce dependence on investment income for solvency
- Investment portfolio governance review (is $830M appropriately allocated and governed?)
- Debt optimization — explore refunding the 2016A bonds (3–5% rates) if market allows
- Self-insurance program review — IBNR growing 28% YoY is a red flag; actuarial refresh recommended
- Covenant compliance monitoring — implement early-warning dashboard
